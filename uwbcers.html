UWBC Exec Recognition System

University of Waterloo Badminton Club (UWBC) depends on its execs to maintain operations and service for the general members. Exec retention rates have experienced a slight decline which poses a future problem in sustaining the club from a management perspective.

The Problem
On the Decline

A 'term' refers to school term which reoccur every 4 months. Every year, there are three terms (Winter, Spring, Fall). The badminton club operates every term and therefore registers a new set of members (up to 300 members) and hires a new exec team (approximately 20 execs and 5 directors) every term.

In short, fewer execs are returning to the club. Execs typically help with setup, cleanup, arranging games, and handling member requests during session; are traditionally compensated with: shirts, social events, food, and an end-of-term dinner. This position is completely voluntary.

Digging into some data

The presented data is based off all the consecutive executive rosters available (terms from 2010 to 2016).

Number of terms of service per exec from 2010 to 2016

Year-to-year retention rates calculated by taking the ratio of returning execs and total execs in year x and year x+1

Key Points:

  • 209 unique execs were hired
  • an exec serves for an average of 2.26 terms
  • 94 of 209 (45%) execs who served the club, only did so for 1 term
  • the retention rate from 2015 to 2016 was 18.2% where only 14 execs returned out of a total of 77 execs

My Role

I was responsible for concept, graphic design, and managing general logistics. This project was mainly individual, but I had help from the rest of the director team to help with recording scores and uploading new profile cards on the leaderboard.

I was a director of the club at the time and I took on this project on my own accord to investigate and explore a real issue.

Pilot
The First Attempt

A pilot was performed in the previous term (Fall 2016). Execs were split into teams and were assigned points for any positive actions throughout the term. This was meant to be fun and slightly competitive to motivate execs (original concept comes from point assignments to different houses in Harry Potter).

Leaderboard from Fall 2016 Term

This system promoted teamwork and was a good 'excuse' to recognize and reward good behaviour. The major issue was the lack of a meaningful scoring system since any director could award points to any exec for practically any reason. It was subjective and bias.

How does it work
Exec Recognition System

The term after (Winter 2017), I proposed a revised system, the Exec Recognition System (ERS). This time, teams would know exactly how they could earn points.

Teams

Teams were balanced based on gender and exec experience.

Teams created their own team name and logo they would represent for the term. The final logos were based on each team's submitted sketch.

Finalized logo for team Keguri

Points

Point entry was done using a Google Form with dropdown menus for exec name, denomination of points, and category (explained in the next section). Lastly, a note must be made describing the exec's actions.

Form used for point entry

All directors were allowed to use this form to enter points and I reviewed the responses regularly to delete any duplicate entries.

Example Entry: "Anna • 5 points • Initiative • First exec to help cleanup"

Categories

Point categories help execs understand how they can earn points for their team. Different categories are represented by badges which can be earned.

Badges

Badges encourage specific behaviour by having achievements for teams to complete (to unlock/upgrade badges).

Explaining the different parts of the badge

Upgrading a badge is symbolized by the medal colour on the bottom right of the badge. A placeholder badge explains what is required to unlock the next badge or badge level.

Bronze, silver and gold medals which can be attached to the badge

Placeholders to explain how teams can earn the next badge or medal
Competition Badge

Exec socials turned into competitive events where they compete to earn a special badge and points.

For this particular term, I organized a relay race and a game show. In the previous term, I organized a pumpkin carving contest and a multi-station challenge.

Badge for winning 3rd place in the relay race

Leaderboard

This displays the team rankings and team profile cards. This was updated every 3-4 weeks.

Profile card contained: team rank, logo, roster, total points, and badges earned. This helped teams visualize their collective achievements and progress.

Team profile card template

Rewards

Mid-Term: The highest point earners from each team was treated to bubble tea.

End-of-Term: The winning team was rewarded an all-expenses paid team dinner.

Summary

Does the exec recognition system (ERS) solve the problem?

Does the ERS address exec retention by recognizing and appreciating execs for their efforts?

Year-to-year retention rates (includes 2016 to 2017 exec data)

Between 2016 and 2017, there was a high of 36.2% retention. Recurring interest in the club exec positon increased, but it would be impossible to say that it was a product of the ERS.

After this experiment, I can say that exec appreciation and recognition goes beyond points, badges, and leaderboards (PBL). The PBL was a fun way to quantitatively track individual exec performance, provide more opportunities to host fun/competitive events, and give out rewards.

Important components of this system includes:

  1. Verbal Feedback: for good and poor performance. Genuine words of affirmation can be worth more than a tangible reward and goes a long way.
  2. Social Events: presented as team competition and demanded teamwork and team cohesion but more importantly, they cultivated a fun environment.

If an exec feels that their efforts are appreciated and at the same time able to create fun memories, I think it's a step forward in retaining execs who are intrinsically motivated to stay term after term.

Conclusion
Last Thoughts

One limitation during the research phase was the ability to diagnose the real reason execs had for discontinuing their service with the available data. It would have been nice to complete exit interviews or start doing exit interviews with execs.

Logistically, other directors mentioned that it was extra effort for them to update points and maintain a system like ERS on top of regular club operations.

The successful components of ERS include: teams, organized exec events, rewards, and feedback. The sense of being a part of a team allow new execs to have a support system to fall upon. Organized exec events should encourage teamwork. Rewards and verbal feedback are also important elements to reassure execs that they are valued. I am not sure how these components will work without PBLs but I look forward to the future of UWBC.
