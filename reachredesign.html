Reach+ Game Redesign

Holland Bloorview Kids Rehabilitation Hospital developed a game interfacing with Microsoft's Xbox Kinect to promote rehabilitation tasks for children with cerebral palsy (CP). They partnered with the University of Waterloo to evaluate, redesign, and provide design recommendations.

Original Game Screens
Creating Confusion

The Kinect system opens up opportunities in gaming to take advantage of whole body movements and even in the field of physical rehabilitation. As opposed to traditional, repetitive, rehabilitation methods, gaming can help boost motivation especially for young children.

The Reach+ game was designed and developed in Holland Bloorview Kids Rehab as part of a Masters thesis. The game promotes arm extension tasks (reaching) and is designed for children with cerebral palsy (CP) who have upper extremity limitations.

The challenge was to evaluate and redesign the experience of game setup, game interaction, and the usability testing protocol.

Game Premise

In this game, the player is tasked with feeding their pet (who is positioned somewhere on the screen). Food items will appear on the screen, and the player must 'reach' to pick them up and deliver them to their pet.

Usability Issues

Therapist

  • lack of flow and linearity in setup
  • elements and descripitions used were unconventional and unclear
  • commonly led to trial and error

Child

  • instructions were heavy with text and unlikely to be read
  • "Why can't I grab the food?"
  • feedback and response was not obvious to reinforce action or promote engaging gameplay
Features
Addressing Usability

The ideation process was based off the usability issues listed in the previous section. We identified key limitations in the current design and brainstormed ideas to address them. We focused on the following key features to help users perform their tasks efficiently.

Linearize Workflow
  • can't start game directly form start screen
  • must have profile loaded to play (and track stats)
  • takes two clicks and a password entry to get into settings
Familiar Settings Menu
  • added concept of presets to reduce setup time
  • large and obvious buttons with a preview of settings when selected
  • edit presets by pressing familiar settings icon
  • intuitive settings adjustment
  • hover over setting to prompt description modal
Giving Child Control
  • allow child and Kinect to orient themselves to each other
  • when child is ready, he/she places hands over ready button to start game
Responsive Feedback
  • original design had poor feedback to know when an appropriate elbow extension angle was achieved
  • coloured overlay about elbow joint
  • when elbow angle is greater or equal to threshold, the overlay turns green and the target object can be grabbed
  • another responsive feedback could include pet emotions when being fed
Usability Testing
Inter-team Peer Assessments

To understand different evaluation methods, we were asked to evaluate our proposed redesign by asking our peers from other teams to complete inspection methods (walkthrough and a heuristic evaluation) to identify usability issues and general feedback. Peer evaluators were familiar with their role as a 'therapist' or a 'child' with respect to the Reach+ game.

Cognitive Walkthrough

Evaluators were asked to go through the wireframes to complete setup and game tasks. They were encouraged to use think-aloud protocol. Comments and observations were recorded while noting any critical incidents.

Heuristic Evaluation

Evaluators were asked to examine the wireframes and check it against a list of heuristics (usability goals). Heuristics were grouped into: visibility of system status, match between system and the real world, user control and freedom, and consistency and standards. The evaluator would check for compliance, record their emotion, and provide any comments.

Heuristic checklist completed by evaluators

Summary of feedback from evaluators:

  • "angle threshold visualization is great"
  • "presets seem like an effective way to minimize setup errors"
  • "some back button controls require rework"
  • "quit button is too close to other buttons"
  • "more game rule description required"

Conclusion
Last Thoughts

This was my first exposure to the design process and wireframing. On top of learning about different research, ideation, and validation methods, I learned how to use wireframes to effectively communicate and express an idea. There was less emphasis on the game screens and game interactions, but I would have liked to further explore UX in the game play and in the Kinect environment.

Limitations

This project had couple limitations regarding the lack of accessibility to 1. real user groups for usability testing and 2. expert evaluators for the walkthroughs and heuristic evaluation methods. We were instructed to complete evaluations by having peers role-play as users or expert evaluators. This project was completed for course credit and having the entire class interview or evaluate real users would potentially overwhelm or stress them.

Usability testing and feedback with a real users is critical for identifying real usability issues and there isn't really a way around this. This project was good practice for following a logical design process but if I could repeat this project outside the contraints of the classroom, I would start with the real users.

Future Considerations

We hope that our design recommendations and findings would be taken into consideration in future development of this game.

We understand that some issues lie beyond UI and usability. The game concept is probably too simple to keep users engaged for a long period of time or subsequent sessions. Additional game mechanics should be explored for feasibility such as: achievement levels aligning with rehabilitation goals, use of other movements, and unlocking new pets.
