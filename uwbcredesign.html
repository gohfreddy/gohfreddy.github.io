UWBC Website Redesign

University of Waterloo Badminton Club (UWBC) organizes and hosts sessions and events for its members. The club's website holds important information but hasn't had a major update for years.

Original Website
Outdated and unengaging

If you want to find out what UWBC is about and what they do, their website is the main source for that. However, the website has not been updated for years and uses outdated stock CSS from the university. User expectations and the nature of how they interact with websites in the present are drastically different compared to ten years ago.

First Impression
  • links are distracting (especially the social media buttons)
  • with nav links on the left and quick links on the right, where do you start?
  • content is condensed and text-heavy
  • level information is linked as a PDF but should have its own page
What Needs Redesign

The content, styling, visual heiarchy and organization needs more love and attention.

One of the first things you notice is how text-heavy the website's content is. Despite being logical (read info to recieve info), there are better methods of delivery (video, images, infographics, etc.) which appeal to the modern web users.

There was also some redundancy of information which fitted across two different pages. Overall, the information architecture and the organization of content needs to be reconsidered.

Exploring Different Layouts
Information Architecture

Looking at the original site and its sitemap, we can see that there are a lot of options on the primary nav which can be overwhelming for new members. The sitemap has a lot of breadth but its individual pages lack depth. The categorization of information should also be reconsidered.

Original Sitemap – Home page linking to 8 different pages

I used card sorting as a tool for me to explore alternative sitemaps. I wrote down 'chunks' of content on cue cards and grouped them into logical groupings to define a form of hieracrchy.

Cart sorting

After formulating various sitemap configurations, I came to this particular version. This revised sitemap added more depth, meaningful labels, and a logical progression along the nav.

Revised Sitemap – Fewer pages and more depth of content

Usability Testing
What Works and what doesn't

InVision was used to create a clickable prototype from the initial mockups. I wanted to evaluate how easy or difficult it was for users to find certain content (based on common tasks).

Users were provided a scenario. For example:

"You don't have any equipment and are unsure of what you're supposed to bring to session or what options you have. Where would you find more information about this?"

Protocol
  1. Present a brief outline of the test and permission to record
  2. Have them fill out general participant information (to categorize into a user group)
  3. Present the prototype
  4. Present scenarios with follow-up questions
    • encourage them to think-aloud
  5. Concluding survey
    • rating appearance and ease of navigation; likes, dislikes, and recommendations
    • allow them to explore the website as normal
Metrics

Duration: time to complete the task

Number of Errors: number of times the user clicked to a different page or went in the opposite direction

Ease: on a scale from 1 (very difficult) to 5 (very easy), the user was asked to rate the task upon completion

Usability test results for task-based scenarios – values are averaged from the number of participants who completed each task

Findings
User group demographics
Likert scale ratings for 'Appearance'
Likert scale ratings for 'Ease of Navigation'

Likes

  • "Clear menu"
  • "Easy to navigate and look for categories"
  • "The more important information stands out"
  • "Simple with open concept"

Dislikes

  • "Might be easier for those who have been in the club before"
  • "Harder to navigate for specific things like challenge rules"
  • "Logic of information placement was not to my expectations"
Summary

Users perform and think differently. Given the same task, individuals may take different approaches or have different mental models for web navigation.

The presented scenarios test users' abilities to find specific information, but some users consume information: by browsing without intentions of finding anything in particular.

There was an overall improvement compared to the original website based on 'appearance' and 'ease of navigation' which scored 4.8 and 4.0 on a 5-point Likert scale respectively (this can be compared to the baseline which scored 2.4 and 3.3 respectively).

Final Design
Mockup, Feedback, Repeat

I have decided to present just the Home page for the final iterations and the Final InVision Prototype.

Aside from the user feedback from testing, I also recieved some feedback from friends who work as product designers and web developers.

Although the initial prototype seemed to be an overall improvement from the original design, the usability test helped me understand what components still needed to addressed. This allowed me progress with further design iteration.

Version 2
Iteration after usability testing and feedback

Summary of Feedback from Users and Professionals:

  • use white text on images (not blue) for readability
  • use a scrim for the navigation bar
  • make tints darker for greater contrast
  • rework the 'How it Works' section since it is important for new members
  • have event cards just link to the Facebook page (easier for maintenance)
  • add more padding to the top and bottom of each section
  • CTA button labels should be clear to let users know exactly where it will lead to

Version 3
Latest iteration of design taking into account feedback

Product design is a never-ending process but I was quite content with this version and how much it evolved from my initial sketches.

The mockup addresses the project goals of an updated look and navigation based on the usability testing results.

Conclusion
A few thoughts

This project was a learning journey for me personally. It allowed me to practice skills like geurilla usability testing and prototyping for the first time. Reading about the design process is one thing but practising it priceless. The feedback from both users and experts also help me understand and see what to consider and pay attention to when designing.

I did a lot of learning on the job, particularly about: colour theory, what information architecture is, how to conduct usability tests, development feasibility, delivering a developer handoff, and much more.

The final proposed design is currently in the development phase. I can't wait for user reactions and how it impacts the future of the club.
